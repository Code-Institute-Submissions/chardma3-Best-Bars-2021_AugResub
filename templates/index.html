DID YOU KNOW?

The word cocktail originated in the United States in the 17th century but didn't gain popularity until the middle of the 1800s. This popularity is thanks in large part to Jerry Thomas, the granddad of mixology, who published his bartender's guide in 1862.

AFTERNOON DEATH

Ernest Hemingway invented this one because of his love of absinthe during his time in France. Death in the Afternoon was also his contribution to “So Red the Nose, or Breath in the Afternoon,” a 1935 cocktail book featuring recipes from 30 celebrity authors. He also wrote the 1932 book by the same name during that period. His recipe for the drink is as follows:


"Pour one jigger absinthe into a Champagne glass. Add iced Champagne until it attains the proper opalescent milkiness. Drink three to five of these slowly"

DID YOU KNOW?

Prohibition in the United States may have banned liquor, but spirits flowed freely in the back alleys, speakeasies, and countrysides of America. In fact, many of the best cocktails came out of and survived those dry years. Some examples of prohibition cockatails are Sidecar, White Lady, Mojito and Daquiri.
